Roof Leaking After a Rainstorm in West Jordan? Here's Exactly What to Do

You hear it before you see it — a drip hitting the ceiling, a water stain spreading across drywall, or worse, water running down an interior wall. If a rainstorm just hit West Jordan and your roof is leaking, you have a narrow window to limit the damage before it gets expensive.

West Jordan sits on the valley floor between the Oquirrh Mountains to the west and the Wasatch Range to the east. When spring and summer storms push through the corridor, rain can be intense and wind-driven — hitting your roof at angles that exploit every weak point. The city's housing mix — ranging from 1980s builds in the neighborhoods east of Redwood Road to brand-new construction in the western developments near the Mountain View Corridor — means the cause of your leak depends a lot on the age and style of your home.

This guide covers what to do in the first hours after a leak, how to identify the likely source, and how Frame Restoration Utah responds to emergency leak calls across West Jordan.

Common Leak Sources in West Jordan Homes

1980s–1990s homes (East West Jordan, near 7800 S / Redwood corridor)

  • Worn pipe boots and vent flashing — Rubber pipe boots around plumbing vents crack and split after 15–20 years. This is the #1 leak source on homes of this age in West Jordan. The boot looks fine from the ground, but the rubber collar has separated from the pipe.
  • Valley flashing failure — Homes with cross-gable roofs rely on metal flashing in the valleys. After 25+ years, galvanized flashing corrodes or shifts, letting wind-driven rain underneath.
  • Nail pops — Thermal cycling (Utah's 100-degree summer-to-winter temperature swings) causes nails to back out of the decking over time. Each popped nail is a potential entry point.

2000s–2010s homes (Central West Jordan, Jordan Landing area)

  • Builder-grade materials aging out — Many homes in this era used 25-year 3-tab shingles and minimum-spec flashing. If your home is 15–20 years old, these materials are reaching end-of-life right when you didn't expect it.
  • Step flashing at sidewalls — Where the roof meets a vertical wall (common on two-story designs), step flashing sealed with caulk instead of properly integrated into the wall can fail when the caulk dries out.

New construction (Western developments, Mountain View Corridor area)

  • Settling-related issues — New homes settle for the first 2–3 years. This can shift flashing, crack caulk seals, and open small gaps at roof-to-wall transitions. Leaks in homes under 3 years old are almost always flashing-related, not shingle-related.
  • Improperly sealed skylights or roof penetrations — Builder installations sometimes cut corners on seal details. The first heavy rainstorm exposes these.

What to Do Right After You Discover a Leak

First 30 minutes — damage control:

  1. Contain the water — Place buckets or bins under active drips. If water is pooling on a ceiling and the drywall is bulging, puncture the center of the bulge with a screwdriver over a bucket to drain it. Letting it spread creates a much larger repair.
  2. Move valuables — Get electronics, furniture, and anything irreplaceable away from the leak zone.
  3. Document everything — Photograph the interior damage (water stains, drips, bulging drywall) and any visible exterior damage. Date-stamped phone photos work. This documentation is critical if you file an insurance claim.
  4. Turn off electricity to affected areas — If water is near light fixtures, outlets, or electrical panels, flip the breaker for that zone. Water and electricity don't mix.

First 2–4 hours — assess and call:

  1. Check the attic if safely accessible — With a flashlight, look for wet rafters, dripping points, or daylight coming through the deck. The point where water enters the attic is often uphill from where it drips through the ceiling — water travels along rafters before dropping.
  2. Call a roofing contractor — Frame Restoration Utah offers emergency leak response in West Jordan. We can typically have a crew on-site same-day for active leaks to install a temporary tarp and stop the water while we plan the permanent fix.
  3. Call your insurance company — Report the leak event. Don't make permanent repairs before the adjuster sees the damage (temporary tarping is fine and expected). In Utah you generally have one year to file, but reporting quickly protects your claim.

Do NOT:

  • Climb onto a wet roof — it's the most common cause of homeowner injury during storms
  • Apply roofing cement or tar from below (it doesn't work and makes professional repair harder)
  • Wait to see if "it stops on its own" — every rainstorm will reactivate the same leak, and the interior damage grows each time

When to DIY vs. Call a Professional

You can handle:

  • Placing buckets and managing interior water
  • Clearing gutters and downspouts that may be contributing to overflow (do this from a ladder, not from the roof)
  • Documenting damage for insurance

Call a professional when:

  • Water is actively entering the home during rain
  • You cannot identify the source from inside the attic
  • The leak appeared suddenly during a storm (likely wind damage or flashing failure — not a DIY fix)
  • Your roof is over 15 years old — the leak you see may be one of several weak points
  • You want to file an insurance claim — a contractor's damage report carries far more weight with adjusters than homeowner descriptions alone

Will my homeowner's insurance cover a roof leak after a rainstorm?

If the leak is caused by a storm event (wind, hail, sudden rain damage), most policies cover it minus your deductible. If the leak is caused by age and wear, insurance typically will not cover it. We help you determine which category your situation falls into before you file.

I see a water stain but it's not currently dripping. Should I still call?

Yes. A stain means water got in at some point, and the entry point is still there. The next rainstorm will reactivate it and the damage zone will grow. It's far cheaper to fix the source now than to repair expanding interior damage later.

My home is brand new (under 3 years old). Can I have a leak already?

Absolutely. New-construction leaks in West Jordan's western developments are almost always related to flashing or seal details at roof penetrations, not the shingles themselves. Your builder's warranty may cover it, but having an independent inspection ensures nothing gets missed. We'll give you a report you can take to the builder.
