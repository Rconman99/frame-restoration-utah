How Professional Emergency Roof Tarping Works

Professional tarping isn't as simple as throwing a tarp over your roof. The process requires skill, experience, and proper materials to ensure the tarp stays secure and protects your home effectively.

Assessment and Damage Evaluation

When Frame Restoration arrives, we first assess the damage extent and severity. We identify all compromised areas and plan the tarping strategy. Large holes require different approaches than widespread shingle loss. We also check for safety hazards—loose branches, structural damage, or unstable sections that could cause injury during work.

Preparing the Roof Surface

Before laying the tarp, we clear debris from the damaged area. Branches, shingles, and other materials are removed so the tarp sits flat and secure. A flat surface is critical—gaps underneath allow water to pool and leak into your home. We also identify attachment points for securing the tarp.

Selecting and Installing the Tarp

Professional-grade tarps are heavier and more durable than consumer versions. We use 6-mil or thicker tarps that withstand wind, sun exposure, and temperature changes. The tarp is sized to cover the damaged area with adequate overlap to secure along the roof edges and extend to gutters.

Installation is where expertise matters. We secure the tarp using:

  • 2x4 lumber: Boards are laid along tarp edges and fastened to the roof with galvanized nails or bolts. This distributes the load and prevents tarp edges from peeling up in wind.
  • Roof battens: Strips of wood are nailed through the tarp into the roof structure, creating secure attachment points every 2-3 feet.
  • Rope or strap systems: Backup securing method that adds redundancy, especially for large tarps or exposed roofs in Layton.

The tarp is stretched tight to shed water toward gutters and downspouts. Slack areas collect water and create leaks, so proper tension is essential. We also ensure the tarp doesn't create safety hazards or damage surrounding structures.

Drainage and Water Management

Even with the best tarp installation, some water may collect on the tarp or at the edges. We sometimes install drainage systems—tubes or gutters that direct water away from vulnerable areas. This extra step prevents water from accumulating and finding its way into the home during heavy rain.

What to Do While Waiting for Permanent Repairs

Emergency tarping is temporary. While your tarp is in place, there are steps you can take to protect your home further and expedite permanent repairs:

Monitor Interior Spaces

Check your attic, ceilings, and walls regularly for water stains or moisture. If you see water damage despite tarping, contact Frame Restoration immediately. The tarp may need adjustment, or water may be entering from an unexpected location.

Document Damage for Insurance

Take photos of the original damage and the tarped area. This documentation supports insurance claims and establishes the timeline of damage. Keep receipts for emergency tarping—this is often covered under homeowners insurance in Layton 84041.

Schedule Permanent Repairs Promptly

Tarping is a short-term solution. Contact Frame Restoration to schedule permanent repairs within days or weeks, not months. The longer a tarp remains in place, the greater the risk of tarp degradation, water infiltration, or secondary damage. Insurance claims require timely repair completion, so don't delay scheduling your permanent roof repair or replacement.

Maintain Gutters and Downspouts

Clear gutters and downspouts to ensure water drains properly away from the tarp and your home's foundation. Clogged gutters cause water to back up under the tarp or overflow near siding and foundation, defeating the purpose of emergency protection.

How Tarping Protects Your Home

You might wonder: what's the big deal about water? Here's why emergency tarping is critical protection:

  • Prevents attic moisture: Wet attic insulation loses R-value, reducing energy efficiency. Moisture encourages mold growth, which causes health issues and expensive remediation.
  • Protects structural components: Wood framing, rafters, and trusses rot when wet. Rot weakens the structure and requires costly replacement. Tarping prevents this damage from starting.
  • Stops drywall damage: Water-damaged drywall warps, discolors, and requires replacement. Multiple ceilings or walls can be affected as water spreads. Tarping prevents the initial intrusion.
  • Protects contents: Water damages furniture, electronics, documents, and personal items. Keeping the interior dry protects your belongings.
  • Prevents mold colonization: Mold begins growing within 24-48 hours of water exposure. Once established, mold remediation is expensive and disruptive. Tarping prevents moisture that enables mold.
  • Maintains home value: Water damage history reduces home value significantly. A tarped roof that's properly repaired maintains your home's market value better than one that suffers extensive water damage.
